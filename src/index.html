The History
I started as an intern working on their iOS app, this is where I learned the ins and outs of product design. We won an app award and I’ve helped the apps team a couple times since then, creating a seamless app experience geared toward the most loyal customers. After a year on apps I moved onto their product navigation page. After joining the team we immediately redesigned the outdated page. It tooks months of research and in addition to creating a cleaner UI and coherent sort/filter system, we were able to cut the load time and improved the search algorithm on the backend. This lead to a long A/B site test and eventual victory over the previous setup in both increasing conversion (~10%) and decreasing bounce rate (~3%).
I started contracting for a few months and continued to help the navigation page while working on some freelance work and personal projects. They offered me to come back full-time and head design on overstock’s Pet adoption site, which was in need of a redesign. After that I moved onto thier real estate sites and did extensive re-design work. In the few years I’ve been at overstock I’ve had the opportunity to touch almost every part of the site and apps, the experience I’ve gained is priceless, both career-wise and personally because I love what I do.
The Process
It’s interesting that in the years of working at overstock my process for designing is always changing and is varies based on the project and timeframe. I don’t beleive there is a clear cut process to follow, because in the end we’re really here simply, to solve problems. Ideally the product manager dev lead and I would be working closely and we’d begin by strategizing together and lean on each other’s experience to come up with the next great idea. Those ealry stages of brainstorming it’s good to have a lot of people involved, especially devs, they know the site better than anyone. It’s also good to have every part of the product mapped out clearly. After an idea comes to light, i might send out a survey and do some competitive analysis to see if consumers think the idea is as important as we think it is. I’ve learned that one of the most important skills is knowing when to say “no” to a project. Too often we are the “yes man” and we get into our “process” for a project that was doomed from the start. Then it’s to the drawing board. Depending on both instinct, data, and user feedback the design takes shape. I like to get into prototypes fairly quickly so I can get that real user feedback as soon as possible. I come from an art background, so I enjoy doing the UI for products myself, but when available it’s good to sync with the visual designer as high-fidelity becomes more necessary. I’ve written numerous user tests, both for in-person and online interviews. The earlier and more efficent the usertest, the better direction you get and more money the company saves. In addition to usertests, we have the luxury at Overstock to A/B site test everything that goes out. So after the user research we can compare the various conversion rates and bounce rates of the actual implementation. Ideally the your user friendly design wins over the original, sometimes it’s a choice between the needs of the business (conversion) versus the needs of the user (clarity, ease of use). It’s important to be with the Front End Developers ensuring the quaility of implementation. Any design, no matter how stunning, is useless unless it is feasable, and can be developed properly. Again, it’s all just problem solving and advocating for the consumer, while keeping the business needs in mind. Get along with others, work hard, and never stop learning.
